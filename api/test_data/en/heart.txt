Most of the modern figurative senses (such as passion or compassion, spirit, inmost feelings, especially love, affection, and courage) were present in Old English. However, the meaning “center” dates from the early 14th century.<ref name="etymonline">{{R:Online Etymology Dictionary}}</ref> The verb sense “to love” is from the 1977 {{w|I Love New York|I ❤ NY}} advertising campaign.<ref name="etymonline"/>

{{col-top|2|Notes on spelling}}
The spelling ⟨ear⟩ for {{IPAchar|/ɑː(ɹ)/}} is paralleled by {{m|en|hearken}} and {{m|en|hearth}}, but is problematic since an Early Modern variant with {{IPAchar|/ɛːr/}} can be posited for those words, but not ''heart''. Perhaps it represents {{m+|gmw-msc|hart}} {{IPAchar|/hɛːrt/}} (reflecting the Scots lengthening of {{IPAchar|/a/}} before {{IPAchar|/r/}} then a consonant, then the early actuation of the {{w|Great Vowel Shift}} in Scots) or a parallel development in Northern England. Alternatively, a back-spelling by speakers of dialects where preconsonantal {{IPAchar|/ɛːr/}} was shortened early, allowing it to undergo the late Middle English lowering to {{IPAchar|/ar/}} (reflected in forms such as {{m|en|larn}} "learn") is possible.
